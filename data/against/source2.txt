AI regulation as currently proposed would entrench existing large technology companies and create insurmountable barriers for startups and smaller competitors. Compliance with complex regulatory requirements requires large legal and engineering teams that only established corporations can afford. The result is regulatory capture — where regulations written with industry input end up protecting incumbents from competition rather than protecting the public from harm.

The definition of AI is so broad and contested that any regulation risks being either too narrow to address real risks or so broad that it captures beneficial software that nobody considers dangerous. Regulating AI as a category is like regulating electricity — the technology itself is neutral, and its risks depend entirely on how it is used in specific contexts.

Self-regulation and industry standards have proven effective in technology sectors. Internet protocols, cybersecurity standards, and software development practices have all evolved through industry collaboration without heavy government intervention. AI ethics boards, model cards, and responsible AI frameworks developed by companies like Google, Microsoft, and Anthropic show that the industry is capable of self-governance.
